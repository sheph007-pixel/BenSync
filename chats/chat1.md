BenSync Website and Brand Strategy Brief

BenSync is a turnkey, bundled employee benefits program built for small employers and distributed primarily through independent insurance brokers, consultants, and agency partners.

The core idea is that small employers need a better benefits solution, but they usually cannot assemble the right vendors, systems, service model, enrollment process, billing structure, and health plan strategy on their own. Independent brokers and agencies also need a better way to deliver a complete benefits solution without building every piece themselves.

BenSync solves that problem by bringing the key components together into one coordinated program.

BenSync is not just a health plan. It is not just a TPA. It is not just enrollment technology. It is the program layer that connects best-in-class vendors and partners into one bundled small-group benefits solution.

The goal is to make a complex benefits ecosystem feel simple:

One program. One bill. One coordinated benefits experience.

What BenSync Is

BenSync is a small-group employee benefits program designed for employers with roughly 2–99 employees.

BenSync assembles and coordinates the pieces so brokers and employers do not have to build the system themselves.

The value proposition is:

BenSync brings the right partners, systems, billing, enrollment, and service model together into one ready-to-use benefits program for small employers.

The Problem We Solve

Small employers want better benefits, lower-cost health plan options, easier enrollment, one bill, and real support for their employees.

But most small employers cannot realistically build this type of program on their own. They would need to separately evaluate and coordinate a TPA, PBM, captive structure, enrollment system, billing process, dental carrier, vision carrier, supplemental carrier, COBRA solution, member app, payment card, service support, and renewal workflow.

Most independent agencies also cannot easily assemble and manage all of those pieces. Even strong agencies may not want to add the staff, infrastructure, and operational burden needed to support a complete benefits platform.

BenSync is built to solve that gap.

It allows brokers and agencies to offer a sophisticated benefits program that has already been assembled, packaged, and operationalized.

Distribution Strategy

Our primary sales channel is independent brokers, consultants, and insurance agencies.

Brokers are not an afterthought. They are our distribution partners.

BenSync should make brokers feel protected, not bypassed. The website should clearly communicate that BenSync helps brokers grow revenue, retain clients, and offer a better solution.

At the same time, employers and members may see the BenSync name in proposals, enrollment materials, benefit guides, bills, apps, or support documents. They may search the name online. So the website cannot be broker-only.

The site needs to serve three audiences:

Employers
They need to understand that BenSync is a complete benefits program for small businesses.

Brokers & Consultants
They need to understand that BenSync is a turnkey solution they can offer to clients, including white-label or co-branded options.

Members
They need a simple place to find support, enrollment help, app access, Paytient information, and benefits guidance.

The main website should position BenSync as the program/platform brand, not as a direct-to-employer brokerage agency.

The site should not feel like “your broker is being replaced.” It should feel like “your broker has access to a better program.”

The layout is clean, but seven nav items is one or two too many, and the fix is trimming, not dropdowns.
No dropdowns. Dropdowns make sense when you have 15+ pages nested under categories. You have roughly seven pages total. At this scale dropdowns hide content, add a click, behave badly on mobile, and make a small site pretend it's a big one. Flat nav is correct for BenSync; revisit only if you later add a resource library or blog.
But trim to five. "What's Included," "The System," and "Partners" are all really one story: what the program is. Three separate top-level slots for it crowds out the audience paths, which are the nav items that actually route visitors. My recommendation:
What's Included | Employers | Brokers &amp; Consultants | Members + Log In + Become a Partner button
Then handle the other two pages like this: "The System" becomes a section on What's Included (the diagram lives mid-page), or a link at the bottom of that page for the people who want the architecture. "Partners" (the vendor network) gets linked from What's Included and the footer. Honestly, the partner network page's most important audience is skeptical brokers and future diligence teams, not casual visitors, so it doesn't need prime nav space. It just needs to exist and be findable.
Two things to fix on what's shown:
The "Log In" link needs a destination decision before launch. Log into what — Employee Navigator? A broker portal? HealthJoy? If different audiences log into different systems, that link should go to a simple chooser page ("Employers/HR: Employee Navigator · Members: HealthJoy · Brokers: Partner Portal"). A login link that dumps a member into a broker portal generates support calls. If there's no live portal yet, cut the link entirely until there is; a dead or confusing login is worse than none.
"Become a Partner" as the header CTA is the right call and consistent with broker-first acquisition, but make it say "Become a Broker Partner" if it fits. Plain "Partner" is ambiguous on a site that also has a vendor "Partners" page two inches to the left. That adjacency will confuse people: same word, two meanings, same header.
Everything else, including the audience order and the button styling, is right.
